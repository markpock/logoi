Martin-Löf's celebrated distinction between judgment and proposition separates the act which confers force from the content which force is conferred on. The proposition $\textsf{``Barry is alive"}$ is a mere object, a dead heap of words which is stitched together by an admissible grammatical-syntactical (analogously -- logical-syntactical) form. The judgment $\textsf{``Barry is alive" true}$ is a (speech) act full of force and vitality -- it asserts something, it commits the speaker to something.

Thus, Martin-Löf distinguishes between the two judgments $\textsf{A prop}$ and $\textsf{A true}$. The former is a judgment still, and it is the judgment which commits us to asserting that what occurs therein ($\textsf{A}$ is a metavariable -- the actual argument) is indeed a proposition; is the sort of thing about which it is even possible to say $\textsf{A true}$ -- that is, $\textsf{A true}$ has as a presupposition that $\textsf{A prop}$.

A pretty story, and one which has had numerous empirical, computational benefits -- for one, judgment forms and their inference rules can be operationalized as decision procedures.

---

Recently, I found myself reading something which mentioned as a stray offhand comment that logic had undergone a shift from Aristotle to today in which previously the judgment form with pride of place was $\textsf{S is P}$ and today is $\textsf{P true.}$ And it is not surprising that things other than propositions can occur as the arguments of judgments -- to be sure, their proofs occur also in the perhaps even more important judgment $\Gamma \vdash \phi : P$.

But it got me thinking -- that the primary judgment form of a logical system should be seen to involve no propositions brings into question the idea that the proposition in the narrow sense of a thing judged $\textsf{A prop}$ may rightly be seen as the content of a judgment in general.

Perhaps this is an obvious point, but if a judgment like $\textsf{S is P}$ is to have any *semantic* content -- quite apart from its *pragmatic* content in the rules of introduction and elimination, then maybe the "confusion" of proposition and judgment is not so wrongheaded after all. For what the classical logic textbook calls a proposition may well just be this *semantic content* of the judgment.

I think it is fair to say that proposition is playing two roles for us here. Insofar as the proposition is a *syntactic* object, whose structure we have insight into -- there, it is dead; there, it needs invigoration by participation in a judgment. The proposition is there akin to a sentence. But insofar as the proposition is a *semantic* object, whose structure we are trying to gain insight into, the proposition is alive, and has all the vitality and determinacy of intension (and intention!). Here, the proposition is akin to a sense. We may say -- proposition as *Satz* is not proposition as *Sinn*. And if it is proposition as *Sinn* that we are interested in -- the determinate content of a judgment apart from its internal pragmatics -- then we can hardly be said to be confusing proposition and judgment at all.

There is a natural retort: "just because we have a name doesn't mean there is an object to which it attaches!" But even more naturally, we should help ourselves to useful theoretical entities (analogously, unobservables -- protons, forces, (provocatively) thoughts). And propositions have no doubt proven useful for logicians in the "algebraic" tradition. More saliently, we should ask the question -- how can we compare judgments? (Or even different judgement systems which present the same calculi). In this sense, "sense" plays the role of "invariant of syntax," as Girard tells us it should.

Perhaps I am missing something very obvious here. But I am becoming less inclined to think that we can do without propositions as full-blooded first-class citizens in logic -- ontology and epistemology in logic go hand in hand, and it is not certain that one should take priority over the other.
